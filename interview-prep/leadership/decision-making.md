# Decision-Making for Senior Architect Interviews

## What Good Decision-Making Sounds Like
Senior architects are expected to make decisions that balance:
- business goals
- technical quality
- delivery speed
- cost
- risk
- team capability

A strong answer shows structured thinking, not just personal preference.

---

## My Decision Framework
When making architecture decisions, use this pattern:
1. clarify the business objective
2. identify constraints and non-functional requirements
3. evaluate realistic options
4. compare trade-offs openly
5. recommend the best-fit option with rationale
6. document the decision and assumptions
7. revisit if the context changes

---

## What Interviewers Want to Hear
- you do not jump to technology choices too early
- you can work through ambiguity
- you explain trade-offs clearly to stakeholders
- you can make pragmatic decisions under time and budget constraints
- you know when to optimize and when to simplify

---

## Sample Interview Answer
### Q: How do you make difficult architecture decisions?
**Tags:** [Leadership] [DecisionMaking] [Architecture] [ArchitectRole]

**Strong answer:**
I start by anchoring the decision to business outcomes and non-functional requirements rather than personal technology preference. Then I typically compare a small number of viable options across criteria such as scalability, security, delivery effort, cost, operational overhead, and future flexibility.

Once the trade-offs are clear, I recommend the best-fit option and make the rationale transparent to both technical and non-technical stakeholders. I also document assumptions because architecture decisions are made in context, and good architects know when to revisit them as that context evolves.

---

## How to Communicate Trade-Offs
Use language like:
- "This option gives us faster delivery, but with less long-term flexibility."
- "This design improves resilience, but it increases operational complexity."
- "Given the current scale and team maturity, I would choose the simpler option first."
- "I would avoid over-engineering now and define clear triggers for future evolution."

---

## STAR Story Template for Decision-Making
### Situation
What decision needed to be made and why it mattered.

### Task
Your role in shaping the outcome.

### Action
How you gathered inputs, aligned stakeholders, and drove the decision.

### Result
Business and technical outcomes.

### Leadership Lens
How you created clarity, confidence, or risk reduction.

---

## Common Decision Scenarios
- monolith vs microservices
- lift-and-shift vs phased modernization
- buy vs build
- speed vs governance
- central platform standards vs team autonomy
- data platform simplification vs maximum flexibility

---

## Mistakes to Avoid
- sounding dogmatic
- talking as if there is always one perfect answer
- not acknowledging business constraints
- presenting decisions without explaining alternatives
- ignoring operational and people impact

---

## Revision Notes
- the strongest decision-makers are transparent, pragmatic, and context-driven
- every decision should sound grounded in outcomes and trade-offs
